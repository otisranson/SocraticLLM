# References

## 1. "To keep AI out of her classroom, this high school English teacher went analog"
**Author:** Lee V. Gaines  
**Source:** NPR (Morning Edition), January 28, 2026  
**URL:** https://www.npr.org/2026/01/28/nx-s1-5631779/ai-schools-teachers-students

Fort Worth high school English teacher Chanea Bond describes her decision to go almost entirely analog — handwritten assignments, daily journaling, no laptops — as the most effective way to keep AI from short-circuiting her students' thinking. When she experimented with allowing AI to generate thesis statements, she found students couldn't engage with the material because they had outsourced the cognitive work. Her grading approach is itself pedagogically telling: she grades each stage of the writing process (outline, draft, bibliography) because "the steps matter" as evidence that thinking is actually happening. The piece also surfaces the counterview from a colleague who advocates modeling responsible AI use, underscoring that the field is still in an experimental phase.

## 2. "What Happened the Year I Banned AI"
**Author:** Chanea Bond  
**Source:** Edutopia, July 29, 2025  
**URL:** https://www.edutopia.org/article/banning-ai-tools-class/

Written by the teacher profiled in the NPR piece above, this first-person account details what actually changed when Bond committed to a full AI ban for a year. Students initially resisted but eventually came to treat their composition notebooks as evidence of their own capability — "holding up their journals as proof that they could achieve difficult tasks." Bond found it easy to detect AI use not through software but through voice: students who used it suddenly sounded generic rather than like themselves. Her core concern is structural: AI reliance, she argues, puts a Band-Aid over skill gaps rather than addressing them, and students from historically marginalized backgrounds are most at risk of losing the foundational development that education is supposed to deliver.

## 3. "AI Backlash Reaches Major University with Bold Ban on Laptops and Phones for Law Students"
**Author:** Julia Bonavita  
**Source:** Fox News (syndicated via MSN), July 2026  
**URL:** https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/ai-backlash-reaches-major-university-with-bold-ban-on-laptops-and-phones-for-law-students/ar-AA27ENWg

Reports on the University of Chicago Law School's announcement that first-year students will be barred from using phones, tablets, and laptops in class beginning fall 2026 — a direct response to AI's growing footprint in legal education. The school's stated rationale is that students must learn to think critically and independently before AI tools are introduced; upper-level courses will then teach responsible, context-aware AI use. The policy frames the sequencing as intentional: build the cognitive foundation first, then teach the technology. Professors may designate "classroom scribes" for note-taking and allow devices for specific activities, but the default is screen-free.

## 4. "University of Chicago Cutting Use of AI by Banning Technology in Classrooms for First-Year Law Students"
**Source:** MSN News, July 2026  
**URL:** https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/university-of-chicago-cutting-use-of-ai-by-banning-technology-in-classrooms-for-first-year-law-students/ar-AA27FSiQ

A companion piece to the Fox News report above, covering the same UChicago Law policy from a different angle and citing Dean Adam Chilton's framing directly. The school's three-part strategic vision — develop AI-resilient pedagogy, cultivate essential human skills that distinguish excellent lawyers, and teach responsible AI use — is outlined here in more detail. The piece notes that legal technology is changing so rapidly that training students only in today's tools may leave them unprepared; the more durable goal is developing the analytical judgment to evaluate AI's advantages and limitations across whatever tools emerge. The school explicitly frames its approach as teaching students to think "with, without, and about AI."

## 5. "Should Schools Ban or Integrate Generative AI in the Classroom?"
**Authors:** Regina Ta and Darrell M. West  
**Source:** Brookings Institution (Center for Technology Innovation), August 7, 2023  
**URL:** https://www.brookings.edu/articles/should-schools-ban-or-integrate-generative-ai-in-the-classroom/

A policy overview mapping the three postures K-12 public schools have taken toward generative AI: banning, integrating, or placing under review. The authors argue that outright bans are a superficial fix — students can circumvent school networks at home — and that banning may actually mask deeper problems with how students are assessed. More importantly, they raise the concern that across-the-board restrictions could limit legitimate educational uses and harm students with learning disabilities who may benefit from AI tools. Their forward-looking recommendations emphasize establishing guiding principles in collaboration with educators and students, providing professional development, and ensuring equitable access so that resource-poor districts are not left behind. The piece is notable for framing the policy question not as ban-vs-embrace but as a governance challenge requiring institutional structure.
